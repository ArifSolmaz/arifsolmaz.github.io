Scientific Premise

Transmission spectroscopy — measuring how transit depth varies with wavelength — is the gold standard for characterizing exoplanet atmospheres, but it requires expensive, dedicated spectroscopic observations (HST, JWST). Meanwhile, hundreds of transiting planets have been observed by multiple photometric surveys operating in different bandpasses: Kepler (420–900 nm, peaked at ~600 nm), TESS (600–1000 nm, peaked at ~800 nm), CHEOPS (350–1100 nm, peaked at ~700 nm), and ground-based surveys like WASP (400–700 nm) and NGTS (520–890 nm). Each mission effectively measures the transit depth at a different effective wavelength.

The idea: by cross-calibrating transit depths measured by different missions for the same planet, one can extract a broadband transmission spectrum — coarse, but covering hundreds of planets simultaneously rather than one at a time. This approach is essentially free, requiring only careful reanalysis of existing public photometry.

Target Datasets

  • Kepler/K2 DR25 light curves — transit depths for ~2,700 confirmed planets.
  • TESS light curves (2-min cadence and FFI) — overlapping observations for ~500+ Kepler planets and ~5,000 TESS-discovered planets.
  • CHEOPS public data archive — targeted observations of ~200 planets with high-precision photometry in a distinct bandpass.
  • Ground-based survey archives (WASP, HATNet/HATSouth, NGTS, TRAPPIST) — transit depths in optical bands.
  • Stellar atmospheric models (PHOENIX, ATLAS9) — needed to compute precise limb-darkening corrections in each bandpass, which is the dominant systematic.

Novelty

Individual studies have noted transit depth differences between Kepler and TESS for specific systems, but no one has performed a systematic, population-level extraction of broadband chromatic transit depth differences across hundreds of planets to map atmospheric properties statistically. The key insight is that while individual depth differences are noisy (~200–500 ppm uncertainty), stacking by planetary class (e.g., hot Jupiters, warm Neptunes, sub-Neptunes) produces a population-averaged transmission signal detectable at high significance.

This is distinct from standard transmission spectroscopy in that it trades spectral resolution for enormous sample size, enabling demographic atmospheric characterization — asking not "what is this planet's atmosphere?" but "do sub-Neptunes as a class have cloud-free or hazy atmospheres?"

Concrete Workflow

  1. Homogeneous transit fitting. For every planet observed by at least two missions, fit transit models independently to each mission's data using identical system parameters (orbital period, inclination, a/R) but allowing the planet-to-star radius ratio R_p/R to float. Use mission-specific limb-darkening coefficients computed from the host star's T_eff, log(g), and [Fe/H] using 3D stellar atmosphere models.
  2. Chromatic depth difference catalog. Compute Δ(R_p/R*)² between each mission pair for every planet. Propagate uncertainties including limb-darkening model uncertainty (the dominant systematic).
  3. Atmospheric model grid comparison. Generate predicted chromatic depth differences from a grid of forward atmospheric models (clear H/He, hazy, cloudy, high-mean-molecular-weight) convolved with each mission's bandpass. Compare to observed Δ(R_p/R*)² values.
  4. Hierarchical Bayesian population analysis. Fit a mixture model to the chromatic depth differences within each planetary class to infer the population-level fraction of clear vs. hazy/cloudy atmospheres.
  5. Validation on known targets. Verify that the method recovers known atmospheric properties for planets with existing HST/JWST transmission spectra (e.g., HD 209458b, WASP-39b, GJ 1214b).

Expected Signal / Observable

A clear, hydrogen-rich atmosphere on a hot Jupiter produces a transit depth difference of ~200–400 ppm between the Kepler and TESS bandpasses (due to Rayleigh scattering slope and Na/K absorption in the bluer Kepler band). For an individual planet, this is marginal (1–2σ). But stacking 50 hot Jupiters yields a population-averaged detection at 7–14σ. For sub-Neptunes, the signal per planet is smaller (~50–100 ppm) but the sample is larger (~300 planets), yielding comparable aggregate significance.

Possible False Positives

  • Limb-darkening mismodeling. If stellar limb-darkening coefficients are incorrect, they produce spurious chromatic depth differences. Mitigation: use 3D MHD stellar atmosphere models rather than 1D; fit limb-darkening from the data for the brightest targets; include limb-darkening uncertainty in the error budget.
  • Stellar contamination / third light. Unresolved background stars contribute differently in each bandpass. Mitigation: Gaia DR3/DR4 provides constraints on nearby contaminants; high-resolution imaging catalogs (Robo-AO, speckle) identify close companions.
  • Starspot-induced chromatic transit depth variations. Unocculted starspots make transits appear deeper in bluer bands regardless of the planet's atmosphere. Mitigation: correlate chromatic depth differences with stellar activity indicators (S-index, photometric variability amplitude); model the starspot contamination spectrum and marginalize over plausible spot coverages.
  • Detector systematics. Bandpass-dependent instrumental effects. Mitigation: the population-level approach averages over instrument-specific biases that vary randomly from target to target.

Why It Matters

JWST can characterize ~50–100 exoplanet atmospheres over its lifetime. This project provides coarse atmospheric demographic information for hundreds of planets simultaneously, answering questions like: What fraction of sub-Neptunes have high-altitude hazes? Does cloud prevalence correlate with equilibrium temperature, surface gravity, or host star metallicity? These population-level answers are inaccessible to JWST's one-at-a-time approach and directly inform atmospheric formation and evolution models. The method also identifies the most promising cloud-free targets for detailed JWST follow-up, improving the efficiency of the most oversubscribed telescope in history.
