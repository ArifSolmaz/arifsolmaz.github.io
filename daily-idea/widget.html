Orbital Decay Rate Anomalies as Probes of Exoplanet Interior Phase Transitions

Tidal orbital decay — the inspiral of a close-in planet due to energy dissipation inside its host star — has been measured for a handful of ultra-short-period planets, most famously WASP-12b (period decrease of ~29 ms/yr). The decay rate depends on the stellar tidal quality factor Q'*, which encodes how efficiently the star dissipates tidal energy. But there is a less-explored flip side: tidal dissipation also occurs inside the planet, and the planetary tidal quality factor Q'_p depends sensitively on the planet's interior structure — specifically, on whether dissipation occurs in a deep convective envelope, a solid/liquid core, or at phase boundaries (e.g., metallic hydrogen transitions, magma ocean surfaces).

The key insight is that Q'_p is not a single number but a frequency-dependent quantity that changes dramatically at interior phase transitions. If a planet's interior is undergoing a gradual structural change (e.g., core erosion, envelope mass loss shifting the metallic-hydrogen boundary), its contribution to orbital evolution changes over time in ways that differ qualitatively from the smooth Q'* decay driven by the star.

Scientific Premise

Tidal orbital decay — the inspiral of a close-in planet due to energy dissipation inside its host star — has been measured for a handful of ultra-short-period planets, most famously WASP-12b (period decrease of ~29 ms/yr). The decay rate depends on the stellar tidal quality factor Q'*, which encodes how efficiently the star dissipates tidal energy. But there is a less-explored flip side: tidal dissipation also occurs inside the planet, and the planetary tidal quality factor Q'_p depends sensitively on the planet's interior structure — specifically, on whether dissipation occurs in a deep convective envelope, a solid/liquid core, or at phase boundaries (e.g., metallic hydrogen transitions, magma ocean surfaces).

The key insight is that Q'_p is not a single number but a frequency-dependent quantity that changes dramatically at interior phase transitions. If a planet's interior is undergoing a gradual structural change (e.g., core erosion, envelope mass loss shifting the metallic-hydrogen boundary), its contribution to orbital evolution changes over time in ways that differ qualitatively from the smooth Q'* decay driven by the star.

Target Datasets

  • Kepler long-cadence transit times for ultra-short-period (USP) planets (P < 1 day) and very hot Jupiters (P < 3 days) — roughly 30 systems with >1,000 individual transits.
  • TESS transit times for the same systems, extending the baseline to 13+ years.
  • Ground-based transit archives (ETD, ExoClock, TRESCA) providing additional mid-transit times spanning 2005–present for the brightest systems.
  • Published radial velocity time series to independently constrain or rule out companions that could mimic decay signatures.
  • JWST secondary eclipse timing (where available) as an independent orbital period measurement at a different orbital phase.

Novelty

Current orbital decay analyses fit a single quadratic (constant dP/dt) to the O–C diagram. This project goes further by searching for deviations from a constant decay rate — cubic or higher-order terms in the O–C curve — which would indicate time-variable tidal dissipation. No systematic search for such higher-order terms has been published.

Additionally, this project reframes orbital decay measurements not merely as probes of stellar interiors (the standard interpretation) but as joint constraints on planetary interior structure. By modeling the coupled star+planet tidal evolution with realistic interior models, one can break the Q'* / Q'_p degeneracy that plagues single-parameter fits, particularly for systems where the planet's contribution to dissipation is non-negligible (e.g., massive hot Jupiters with deep convective envelopes, or rocky USP planets with magma oceans).

Concrete Workflow

  1. Comprehensive timing catalog. Assemble all published and archival mid-transit times for ~30 USP and very-hot-Jupiter systems. Re-extract Kepler and TESS transit times with uniform methodology. Cross-validate with ground-based archives.
  2. Polynomial O–C fitting. For each system, fit O–C models of increasing complexity: linear (constant period), quadratic (constant dP/dt), cubic (linearly changing dP/dt). Use Bayesian information criterion to identify systems where the cubic term is statistically justified.
  3. Coupled tidal evolution modeling. For systems with significant cubic terms, run coupled star-planet tidal evolution models using the MESA stellar evolution code (for Q'* as a function of stellar structure and age) and interior structure models (for Q'_p as a function of planetary mass, radius, composition, and thermal state). Explore whether the observed decay rate curvature is consistent with planetary interior evolution (core erosion, thermal contraction) or requires invoking stellar evolution alone.
  4. Population constraints on Q'_p. For systems without significant cubic terms, the upper limits on decay rate variation still constrain Q'_p and its frequency dependence. Combine these into a population-level inference on the distribution of planetary tidal quality factors as a function of planet mass, radius, and equilibrium temperature.
  5. Forward predictions. For the most promising systems, predict the expected O–C curvature over the next 5–10 years and identify the observations most likely to distinguish between planetary and stellar dissipation hypotheses.

Expected Signal / Observable

For WASP-12b (the best-characterized decaying system), a 10% change in effective Q' over the 18-year observational baseline produces a cubic O–C term of ~5–15 seconds at the endpoints, detectable at 2–4σ given current timing precision (~10 s per epoch from TESS, ~3 s from ground-based facilities). For rocky USP planets like Kepler-78b, the planetary tidal contribution is proportionally larger (rock dissipates energy more efficiently than gas), and magma ocean dynamics could produce detectable O–C curvature on similar timescales.

Possible False Positives

  • Rømer delay from unseen companions. A long-period companion causes a light-travel-time effect that mimics a cubic O–C term. Mitigation: radial velocities constrain companions; the Rømer effect is strictly periodic, not monotonic.
  • Stellar magnetic activity cycles. Stellar activity can subtly modulate the apparent transit times via the starspot-crossing mechanism. If the activity cycle period is comparable to the observational baseline, this produces curvature in the O–C diagram. Mitigation: correlate O–C residuals with stellar activity proxies (S-index, photometric variability); model and subtract the expected activity-induced timing offset.
  • Applegate mechanism. Magnetic activity-driven changes in the star's quadrupole moment modulate the orbital period. Mitigation: the Applegate effect produces periodic, not secular, O–C variations; it also predicts correlated changes in transit duration, which can be tested.
  • Apsidal precession. For eccentric orbits, apsidal precession produces O–C curvature. Mitigation: most USP planets have circularized orbits; for the few with residual eccentricity, apsidal precession also produces predictable TDV patterns.

Why It Matters

Planetary interior structure remains one of the least constrained properties of exoplanets. Mass and radius give mean density, but degeneracies between composition and structure are severe. Tidal dissipation provides an independent window into interior properties because Q'_p depends on the planet's rheology, phase state, and layered structure in ways that mass and radius alone cannot capture. Detecting time-variable orbital decay would be the first direct evidence for evolving planetary interiors — a fundamentally new observable in exoplanet science. Even non-detections yield the first population-level constraints on planetary Q' values, with implications for the long-term orbital stability of close-in planetary systems and the ultimate fate of hot Jupiters.
