Mapping Exoplanet Period–Radius Deserts Across Galactic Stellar Populations Using Kepler + Gaia Chemodynamics

Scientific Premise

The well-known features in the exoplanet period–radius diagram — the hot Neptune desert, the radius valley, the sub-Jovian savanna — are shaped by formation, migration, and atmospheric evolution processes. However, nearly all demographic studies treat the host star population as monolithic, or at best split by spectral type. With Gaia DR3 providing precise kinematics, metallicities, and ages for nearly all Kepler host stars, it is now possible to ask: do the boundaries of the hot Neptune desert and radius valley shift systematically between distinct Galactic stellar populations (thin disk, thick disk, high-α vs. low-α sequences)? These populations differ in age, metallicity, and α-element enrichment, each of which independently affects planet formation (disk mass, solid inventory, migration timescales) and atmospheric evolution (XUV irradiation history, which correlates with stellar age).

Target Datasets

  • Kepler DR25 planet candidate catalog and confirmed planet catalog (~4,000 candidates with well-characterized completeness).
  • Gaia DR3 astrometry (parallaxes, proper motions) and spectroscopy (radial velocities, [M/H], [α/Fe]) for Kepler target stars.
  • LAMOST DR9 / APOGEE DR17 for higher-precision chemical abundances where Gaia's spectroscopic pipeline has limitations.
  • Kepler stellar properties catalog (Berger et al. 2020) updated with Gaia-based radii for homogeneous planet radius calculation.
  • Kepler completeness maps (e.g., from the DR25 completeness products or Burke et al. 2015) for occurrence rate calculations.

Novelty

While the dependence of planet occurrence on metallicity is well-studied (the giant planet–metallicity correlation), and some work has explored occurrence vs. age (using gyrochronology or isochrone ages), the simultaneous use of full chemodynamic population membership (thin disk vs. thick disk, [α/Fe] sequences) as an independent variable for sculpting the topology of the period–radius diagram is underexplored. This goes beyond asking "do old stars have fewer planets?" to asking "does the hot Neptune desert have a different boundary for thick-disk (old, α-enhanced) stars vs. thin-disk (younger, solar-α) stars?" — which probes whether the desert is primarily sculpted by formation conditions (correlated with birth environment/metallicity) or post-formation evolution (correlated with age/XUV history). The Gaia+spectroscopic survey combination makes this possible for the first time at scale.

Concrete Workflow

  1. Stellar Population Classification: Cross-match Kepler targets with Gaia DR3 + LAMOST/APOGEE. Classify stars into chemodynamic populations using [α/Fe] vs. [Fe/H] (the bimodal α-sequence), supplemented by kinematic criteria (Galactic velocity dispersions). Assign population membership probabilities.
  2. Homogeneous Planet Parameters: Recompute planet radii using Gaia-based stellar radii. Apply uniform detection efficiency corrections using the Kepler completeness maps.
  3. Population-Resolved Occurrence Rates: Compute planet occurrence rates in the period–radius plane separately for each stellar population, using inverse-detection-efficiency weighting. Use ~15 × 15 bins in log-period vs. log-radius space.
  4. Desert/Valley Boundary Fitting: For each population, fit the boundaries of the hot Neptune desert (upper and lower edges in the period–radius plane) and the radius valley (the gap between super-Earths and sub-Neptunes) using a parametric model (e.g., power-law boundaries) or a non-parametric kernel density estimate. Compare boundary locations across populations.
  5. Statistical Significance: Use bootstrap resampling and hierarchical Bayesian modeling to assess whether observed boundary shifts exceed what is expected from sample size differences alone. Test against null models where population membership is randomized.

Expected Signal / Observable

If the hot Neptune desert is primarily shaped by XUV-driven photoevaporation (age-dependent), thick-disk planets (older by ~4–8 Gyr on average) should show a wider desert extending to larger planetary radii, because more atmospheric mass has been stripped. Conversely, if the desert is shaped by formation/migration (metallicity-dependent), the desert boundaries should shift with [Fe/H] but not independently with age at fixed metallicity. The radius valley position is predicted to scale as R_valley ∝ F^α where F is the incident flux; for older (lower-luminosity?) stars the valley may appear at a different radius. Expected shifts: ~0.1–0.3 R⊕ in the valley position and ~10–30% changes in the desert boundary period, based on photoevaporation model predictions.

Possible False Positives

  • Detection bias correlation with stellar population: Thick-disk stars are on average more distant and fainter in the Kepler field, leading to lower detection completeness for small planets. Mitigation: explicitly model and correct for detection completeness as a function of stellar magnitude and planet size; restrict analysis to magnitude-limited subsamples where completeness is comparable.
  • Small sample size for thick disk: Only ~10–15% of Kepler targets are thick-disk stars, yielding ~300–500 planet candidates. Mitigation: use hierarchical models that borrow strength across populations; focus on the most robust features (desert vs. non-desert) rather than fine structure.
  • Age–metallicity degeneracy: Thick-disk stars are both older and more α-enhanced, making it hard to separate age from chemistry. Mitigation: include [Fe/H]-matched subsamples across populations, and use gyrochronological or asteroseismic ages for the subset with such measurements to partially break the degeneracy.

Why This Matters

This project connects exoplanet demographics to Galactic archaeology — asking whether the planetary systems formed in the Milky Way's early thick disk differ architecturally from those formed more recently in the thin disk. A positive detection of population-dependent desert/valley boundaries would (a) discriminate between photoevaporation and core-powered mass loss as the dominant atmospheric sculpting mechanism (they predict different age dependencies), (b) provide a new "chemical clock" approach to understanding planet formation timescales, and (c) open the door to using planet demographics as a tracer of Galactic chemical evolution, complementing traditional stellar archaeology.
