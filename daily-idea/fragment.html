Scientific Premise

Trojan companions — bodies librating around the L4/L5 Lagrange points of a planet — are ubiquitous in our solar system (Jupiter alone hosts over 12,000), yet not a single confirmed exo-Trojan exists. Most searches have focused on hot Jupiters, where tidal migration likely destabilizes co-orbitals. The recent ExoEcho study (Barragán et al. 2026) constrained Trojans for 260 hot Jupiters using TESS TTVs alone but found no detections — an expected null given the dynamical hostility of those orbits.

The underexplored sweet spot is warm Jupiters (P ~ 10–200 days). These planets underwent less violent migration, and dynamical simulations predict that co-orbital trapping during disk-driven migration is most efficient at intermediate orbital distances. Yet warm Jupiters have sparse TESS coverage (often only a handful of transits) and their Kepler-era TTVs were never systematically searched for the specific libration-period signature of a Trojan.

Target Datasets

  • Kepler DR25 long-cadence light curves for all confirmed warm Jupiters (roughly 50–80 systems with P > 10 d and M_p > 0.3 M_Jup).
  • TESS FFI light curves (Sectors 1–83, through the extended mission) for the same targets, providing a 7–9 year lever arm when combined with Kepler epochs.
  • Published radial velocity datasets from the California Legacy Survey, HARPS archive, and others to constrain planetary masses and break TTV degeneracies.
  • Gaia DR4 astrometry (expected 2026) for independent companion-mass upper limits in wide-orbit cases.

Novelty

Previous TTV-based Trojan searches used either Kepler or TESS data in isolation. Combining both missions extends the observational baseline from ~4 years (Kepler alone) to ~13 years, which is critical because Trojan libration periods scale as P_lib ∝ P_orb / √(m_Trojan/m_star) and can be decades long for Earth-mass Trojans around warm Jupiters. A 13-year baseline makes sub-cycle curvature detectable for the first time in many systems.

Furthermore, while the ExoEcho project targeted hot Jupiters (where theory predicts Trojan instability), this project specifically targets the dynamically favorable warm-Jupiter regime — a population large enough for statistical constraints but neglected in systematic Trojan searches.

Concrete Workflow

  1. Uniform transit timing extraction. Re-fit all Kepler long-cadence and TESS 2-min/FFI transits for the warm Jupiter sample using a consistent model (e.g., batman + GP stellar variability). Produce a homogeneous O–C (observed minus calculated) timing catalog.
  2. Trojan TTV model injection-recovery. Simulate TTV signals from co-orbital companions across a grid of Trojan masses (0.1 M_Earth to 10 M_Earth) and libration amplitudes (20°–60°). Inject into real O–C residuals to quantify detection sensitivity per system.
  3. Bayesian model comparison. For each system, fit three models to the O–C data: (a) linear ephemeris, (b) sinusoidal TTV from a perturbing planet in mean-motion resonance, (c) Trojan libration TTV (which has a characteristic period and amplitude relationship distinct from generic TTVs). Use nested sampling to compute Bayes factors.
  4. Population-level upper limits. Even non-detections yield a joint upper limit on the occurrence rate of massive Trojans around warm Jupiters — a quantity with direct implications for migration theory.
  5. Follow-up prioritization. Flag any candidates with Bayes factor > 10 in favor of the Trojan model for targeted JWST or ground-based transit photometry at predicted libration-maximum epochs.

Expected Signal / Observable

A 1 M_Earth Trojan around a warm Jupiter (P = 30 d, M_p = 1 M_Jup) produces TTV amplitudes of ~30–90 seconds with a libration period of ~15–25 years. Over a 13-year Kepler+TESS baseline, this manifests as a slow, monotonic or gently curving O–C drift distinguishable from a linear ephemeris at the 3–5σ level for systems with individual timing precisions of ~30 s.

Possible False Positives

  • Apsidal precession from an unseen outer companion mimics a slow TTV drift. Mitigation: radial velocity data constrain outer companions; apsidal precession also produces correlated transit duration variations (TDVs), which Trojan TTVs do not.
  • Stellar activity–induced timing biases. Starspot crossings during transit shift the measured mid-time. Mitigation: GP detrending of in-transit residuals; consistency checks between Kepler short-cadence and long-cadence timings.
  • Instrumental systematics. Kepler-to-TESS cross-calibration offsets in timing zero-points. Mitigation: ground-based transit observations (e.g., from ExoClock) serve as an independent bridge between missions.

Why It Matters

Detecting or placing stringent upper limits on exo-Trojans around warm Jupiters directly tests predictions of disk-driven migration models. A detection would be the first confirmed exo-Trojan — a landmark discovery. Even a robust null result constrains the efficiency of co-orbital capture and the prevalence of material at planetary Lagrange points, informing models of late-stage planet formation and the delivery of volatiles.
