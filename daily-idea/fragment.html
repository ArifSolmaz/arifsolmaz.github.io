Detecting "Ghost Companions" in Single-Transiting Kepler Systems via Secular TTV Drift and Host Star Refractory Abundance Anomalies

The Kepler mission discovered thousands of multi-planet systems, but a large fraction of Kepler planets (~45%) appear to be "singles" — systems with only one detected transiting planet. This singles excess has been called the "Kepler Dichotomy" and remains one of the outstanding puzzles in exoplanet demographics. One leading hypothesis is that many singles once had companions that were dynamically ejected or engulfed by the host star through planet-planet scattering events. Scientific Premise

The Kepler mission discovered thousands of multi-planet systems, but a large fraction of Kepler planets (~45%) appear to be "singles" — systems with only one detected transiting planet. This singles excess has been called the "Kepler Dichotomy" and remains one of the outstanding puzzles in exoplanet demographics. One leading hypothesis is that many singles once had companions that were dynamically ejected or engulfed by the host star through planet-planet scattering events. If true, these disrupted systems should carry two independent forensic signatures: (a) secular transit timing variation (TTV) drifts — long-term, non-periodic changes in the transit ephemeris caused by the decaying dynamical perturbation of a recently lost companion, and (b) refractory element enrichment in the host star's photosphere if the companion was consumed.

This project proposes to cross-correlate these two independent lines of evidence across the full Kepler sample to identify candidate "recently disrupted" systems and constrain the rate of late-stage planetary system instability.

Target Datasets

  • Kepler long-cadence photometry (Q0–Q17, ~4 years) for all ~2,300 confirmed single-transiting planets. Transit times extracted via individual transit fitting (not phase-folded) to preserve any long-term drift signal.
  • TESS extended mission data for the subset of Kepler singles in the TESS continuous viewing zone, extending the TTV baseline to ~15 years (2009–2024+).
  • Spectroscopic abundance catalogs: GALAH DR4 (~600,000 stars, 30 elements), APOGEE DR17, California-Kepler Survey (CKS), and the Brewer et al. (2016) spectroscopic catalog specifically targeting Kepler host stars. Focus on refractory-to-volatile abundance ratios ([Fe/H]–[C/H], [Si/Fe], [Ti/Fe]) and differential analysis relative to binary companions or co-moving pairs.

Novelty

TTV studies overwhelmingly focus on periodic signals from known or suspected companion planets in multi-transiting systems. Secular (monotonic or slowly curving) TTV drifts in single-planet systems are usually discarded as systematic errors or treated as evidence for long-period non-transiting companions still present in the system. This project specifically targets decaying secular drifts — the dynamical "ringing down" after a companion has been lost — which is a qualitatively different signal that has been discussed theoretically (e.g., Volk & Gladman 2015 on solar system instability aftermath) but never systematically searched for in Kepler data.

Separately, refractory enrichment of planet-hosting stars has been studied (Meléndez et al. 2009; Bedell et al. 2018; Spina et al. 2021), but always as a statistical signal across populations. No one has attempted to identify individual systems where both the dynamical and chemical signatures of recent disruption are present simultaneously. The conjunction of these two independent diagnostics dramatically increases confidence and reduces false positives.

Concrete Workflow

  1. TTV extraction: Fit individual transit times for all ~2,300 Kepler singles using a wavelet-based detrending + MCMC transit model. For targets also observed by TESS, append the TESS-epoch transit times to extend the baseline.
  2. Secular drift detection: Fit each TTV time series with three models: (a) constant period (null), (b) constant period derivative (linear drift, e.g., from tidal decay or apsidal precession), (c) exponentially decaying drift (post-instability relaxation, τ ~ 10^3–10^5 years). Use Bayesian evidence (via MultiNest) to select among models.
  3. Chemical tagging: For each Kepler single host star with available high-resolution spectroscopy, compute the differential refractory-to-volatile ratio [X_refrac/X_vol] relative to: (a) the galactic chemical evolution trend at that [Fe/H] and age, and (b) co-moving stellar companions if available (e.g., from Gaia wide binary catalogs). Flag stars with >2σ refractory enrichment.
  4. Cross-correlation: Identify systems that show both a significant decaying TTV drift and anomalous refractory enrichment. Estimate the expected coincidence rate from random overlap (shuffled associations) to determine if the correlation is statistically significant above chance.
  5. Dynamical modeling: For the strongest candidates, run N-body scattering simulations (REBOUND/WHFast) to test whether a recent instability event can reproduce both the observed TTV drift amplitude/timescale and the implied mass of engulfed material.

Expected Signal / Observable

Post-instability TTV drifts are expected to have amplitudes of ~10–100 seconds over the 4-year Kepler baseline for disruption events occurring within the last ~10^4–10^5 years, decaying roughly exponentially. Kepler's timing precision for typical singles (~1–4 R_Earth, P ~ 1–10 days) is ~10–60 seconds per transit, so the signal is marginal per-transit but detectable as a coherent trend across ~100+ transits. Refractory enrichment from ingesting a ~1–10 M_Earth rocky planet corresponds to [Fe/H] enhancements of ~0.01–0.05 dex for a solar-type star, detectable with high-precision differential spectroscopy (σ ~ 0.01 dex achieved by CKS and GALAH for bright targets). The key prediction: systems with decaying TTV drifts should show refractory enrichment at a rate significantly exceeding the background, and the enrichment amplitude should correlate with the inferred companion mass from the TTV drift.

Possible False Positives

  • Tidal orbital decay produces a linear (not decaying) TTV drift and is only significant for very short-period planets (P < 1 day). Mitigation: the exponential decay model is distinguishable from linear drift with sufficient baseline; exclude USP planets.
  • Apsidal precession from a still-present companion produces secular TTV drift without any disruption. Mitigation: search for radial velocity trends or astrometric accelerations (Gaia DR4) that would reveal an extant companion.
  • Galactic chemical evolution scatter can mimic refractory enrichment. Mitigation: use differential analysis relative to co-moving wide binary companions (same natal cloud, same age) to remove GCE effects; this technique has been demonstrated to reach ~0.01 dex precision.
  • Convective mixing timescale: Refractory enrichment from engulfment fades as the convective envelope mixes. For solar-type stars, this timescale is ~10^7–10^8 years, so only "recent" events (consistent with the TTV timescale) would show chemical signatures. This is actually a feature, not a bug — it provides a natural consistency check.

Why It Matters

The rate of late-stage dynamical instability in planetary systems is a fundamental but poorly constrained parameter in planet formation theory. It determines how many potentially habitable systems self-destruct after formation, affecting occurrence rate estimates for Earth-like planets. Identifying individual recently disrupted systems — not just statistical trends — would provide direct observational evidence for the process and calibrate N-body instability models. Moreover, if the Kepler Dichotomy is partly driven by dynamical instability, this project would quantify what fraction of "singles" are actually the survivors of once-richer systems, fundamentally reshaping our understanding of the exoplanet census.
